Old Fashioned Cherry Pie

By Mary Ringlein

Tart cherry pie has been an American classic since at least the 19th century, when sour cherries — grown abundantly in Michigan, Wisconsin, and Ohio — became the standard for pie filling over sweet varieties. The filling here is cooked on the stovetop first, thickened with flour and the juice from the can, then brightened with almond extract and a touch of red food coloring before going into the crust. That stovetop step is the old-fashioned hallmark: it ensures the filling is perfectly set when the pie comes out of the oven, with no risk of a soggy or runny center. Mary Ringlein’s lattice top lets the ruby filling show through, and the two-stage bake — a blast of heat to set the crust, then a long gentle finish — produces golden, flaky edges every time.

Ingredients

  • 1 c sugar
  • ¼ c flour
  • ¼ tsp salt
  • ½ c juice from canned cherries
  • 3 c canned, drained, pitted tart cherries
  • 10 drops red food coloring
  • 1 Tbsp butter, softened
  • 4 drops almond extract
  • Pastry for a 9-inch lattice pie (top and bottom crust)

Instructions

  1. In a medium saucepan, combine the sugar, flour, and salt. Stir in the cherry juice.
  2. Cook and stir over medium heat until the mixture thickens. Cook for 1 minute longer.
  3. Remove from heat. Stir in the drained cherries, butter, almond extract, and red food coloring.
  4. Let the filling stand while you prepare the pastry.
  5. Preheat oven to 450°F.
  6. Line a 9-inch pie plate with the bottom crust. Pour in the cherry filling.
  7. Top with a lattice crust. Flute the edges to seal.
  8. Bake at 450°F for 10 minutes.
  9. Reduce heat to 350°F and bake for 45 minutes more, until the crust is golden brown and the filling is bubbling.
  10. Cool completely before slicing.

For the crust: See Mary Ringlein’s Pie Crust for the pastry. Her two-stage shortening technique makes a flaky, tender crust that holds up beautifully to a lattice top.

Tart vs. sweet cherries: This recipe calls for tart (sour) cherries, not sweet cherries. Look for canned tart red cherries — they hold their shape and flavor through cooking far better than sweet varieties.
