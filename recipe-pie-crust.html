Pie Crust

From the kitchen of Mary Ringlein
← Back to All Recipes

Crisco vegetable shortening transformed American home baking when it launched in 1911, and by the mid-20th century the shortening pie crust had become the standard in kitchens across the country. Before it, most home bakers used lard, which produced a flavorful but variable crust depending on the quality of the fat. Shortening was consistent, affordable, and forgiving — and it produced a reliably tender, flaky crust every time. Mary Ringlein’s recipe includes a technique not found in most modern versions: dividing the shortening in half and cutting each portion in separately. The first half is worked in until the mixture resembles cornmeal, which creates a tender base; the second half is left as small pea-sized pieces, which create the flaky layers. It’s an old-fashioned baker’s trick that makes a genuinely better crust. Makes enough for one double-crust pie or two single-crust pies.

Ingredients

  • 2 c sifted all-purpose flour
  • 1 tsp salt
  • ⅓ c shortening
  • 5–7 Tbsp cold water

Instructions

  1. Sift together flour and salt into a medium bowl.
  2. For an extra tender and flaky crust, divide the shortening in half. Cut in the first half with a pastry blender until the mixture resembles cornmeal. Then cut in the remaining half until the pieces are the size of small peas. (Alternatively, cut all shortening in at once until pieces are pea-sized.)
  3. Sprinkle 1 Tbsp cold water over part of the flour mixture. Gently toss with a fork, then push to one side. Repeat with remaining water, 1 Tbsp at a time, over the dry parts until all the flour is just moistened. Do not overwork.
  4. Gather dough with fingers and form into a ball. For a 2-crust pie, divide in half and roll each into a ball.
  5. On a lightly floured surface, flatten the ball slightly and roll to ⅛” thick, always rolling lightly from center to edge.
  6. Transfer to a 9” or 10” pie plate. Tip: for a flakier crust, wrap the dough and refrigerate for 30 minutes before rolling.
